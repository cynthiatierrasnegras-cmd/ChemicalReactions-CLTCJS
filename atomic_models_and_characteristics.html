Part 1 — Atomic Models Timeline Session 1 · 35 min

Each card below represents a milestone in our understanding of the atom. Read, discuss with your partner, then answer the reflection questions.

1803
John Dalton

Solid Sphere Model

Dalton proposed that all matter is made of tiny, indivisible spheres called atoms. Atoms of the same element are identical; atoms combine in fixed ratios to form compounds.

💡 Key idea: Atoms are solid, indestructible, and cannot be created or destroyed — only rearranged.
1897
J.J. Thomson

Plum Pudding Model

Thomson discovered the electron through cathode ray experiments. He proposed atoms are a diffuse positive cloud with negative electrons embedded throughout — like raisins in pudding.

💡 Key idea: Atoms contain smaller, negative particles. Atoms are divisible.
1911
Ernest Rutherford

Nuclear Model

Rutherford's gold-foil experiment revealed that most of an atom is empty space, with a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center. Electrons orbit far from the nucleus.

💡 Key idea: Most atomic mass is concentrated in a tiny nucleus. Atoms are mostly empty.
1913
Niels Bohr

Planetary Model

Bohr proposed electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed energy levels (shells). Electrons can jump between levels by absorbing or emitting specific amounts of energy (quanta).

💡 Key idea: Electron energy is quantized — electrons occupy defined energy shells. (Basis for electron configuration!)
1926
Schrödinger / Heisenberg

Quantum Mechanical Model

Electrons do not travel in fixed paths. Instead, they exist in probability clouds called orbitals — regions where an electron is most likely to be found. This is the model used today.

💡 Key idea: We cannot know the exact position of an electron — only its probability. Orbitals replace orbits.

🔢 Atomic Number & Mass Number
Z
Atomic Number
= Number of protons in the nucleus
  • Unique to each element
  • Determines the element's identity
  • In a neutral atom: Z = number of electrons
Na → Z = 11 → 11 protons, 11 electrons
A
Mass Number
= Number of protons + neutrons
  • Also called nucleon number
  • Electrons contribute negligible mass
  • Neutrons = A − Z
Na-23 → A = 23, Z = 11 → 12 neutrons
AZ X
Nuclide Notation
Standard way to write isotopes
  • X = chemical symbol
  • Top left = Mass number (A)
  • Bottom left = Atomic number (Z)
2311Na  =  Sodium-23
